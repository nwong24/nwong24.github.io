### How I made my estimates
I made my estimates by comparing the task to anything similar I did in the past. If it was something small, such as updating a Prisma schema, I could estimate that it would only take a few minutes. However, if it was something like creating a UI with React, I knew I would struggle more because I am not so familiar and estimated that it would take more time. Some useful points of reference I used were from the effort estimation logs I kept from the practice WODs we did. I usually added a bit more time to similar tasks to account for the lack of a guide to help me through the task.

### How useful is tracking effort?
Tracking effort was useful because it allowed me to gauge areas I was lacking in, allowing me to choose tasks that were better suited for my skillset. It also allowed me to make better informed estimates for tasks I did afterwards. My tool of choice for tracking effort was just using a stopwatch. Whenever I had to take a break and step away from the task, I paused the time. This was simple, but it got the job done effectively.

### If I could do it all over again
Some advice I would give my past self is to consider the things that still had to be done outside of writing code. For example, when we were first coming up with concepts for our site, we decided to add a way for users to see how busy an area was in the past. However, we also had to decide how this system would work. While doing the task to create the system, I had to spend a little time considering this, which made it take longer than just writing the code.

### Using AI
When estimating some tasks, I would ask OpenAI's ChatGPT how long it thought a junior developer would take to complete a task like *some task*. Sometimes tasks that I thought were simple would have a much longer suggested time than I thought. I usually just took its feedback into account and accordingly increased my estimated time.
